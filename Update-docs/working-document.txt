Basileian Canon
A Chronological Harmony
The passages are arranged in the approximate order in which they happened in Jesus's life and ministry. Where the Gospels (and the non-canonical sources) report the same event or saying, the parallels are grouped on one line. "//" separates parallel accounts. Bracketed items are the non-NT or epistolary sources folded in where they fit.
Two notes. First, the order is a scholarly harmonization, not something the Gospels agree on — the Synoptics and John diverge sharply on sequence (most visibly the temple cleansing and the number of Passovers). Disputed placements are flagged. Second, grouping parallels does not assert they are the same event; distinct-but-similar episodes (the two feedings, the two anointings) are grouped under a shared heading with a note.

Editor's Note on Non-Canonical Insertions (READ FIRST)
Every place a non-canonical text belongs is now marked with an explicit directive so the app build can insert deterministically. Three markers are used:
▶ INSERT — material to fold in at this pericope. Each directive names the source, the exact unit (logion number / chapter:verse / fragment + lines / section range), whether the whole unit or a specified extent is taken, and the source location of the text.
✕ EXCLUDE — material from a source that is not admitted at this point, with the reason.
⚑ DECISION — a genuine editorial choice left open for you; not silently resolved.
Where the source text lives (used verbatim in every directive):
Thomas PDF — Gospel_of_Thomas_Lambdin.pdf (logia numbered 1–114).
Didache PDF — Didache_-_Roberts.pdf (chapters 1–16).
In-doc — full text is in this document, either under "Non-Biblical text sources" (P.Oxy 1224; Codex Bezae addition) or under "Non-Canonical Texts" (P.Oxy 5575; Gospel of Peter; Gospel of the Hebrews; P.Oxy 840; 1 Clement 13; Polycarp, Phil. 2:3; Egerton Gospel).

What Has Been Left Out
The Basileian Canon begins from the New Testament and admits only what reports the ministry, death, resurrection, and teaching of Jesus as fact, from the start of the public ministry down to the ascension (the terminus). Whole books and large blocks of the New Testament therefore fall outside it. Their omission here is a statement about this canon's reporting criterion, not a verdict on their standing in the wider New Testament: the material below is left out because it is pre-ministry, post-ascension, or interpretation rather than narrated report — not because it is judged spurious. Where an otherwise-excluded book transmits a saying or formula that does report Jesus as fact, that fragment is admitted at its place in the harmony and noted below.
1. Pre-ministry material
Excluded as preceding the canon's starting point (John the Baptist's preaching, Part I).
Matthew's infancy narrative and genealogy — Matt 1:1–2:23 (genealogy, nativity, Magi, flight to Egypt, Herod).
Luke's infancy narrative and genealogy — Luke 1:1–2:40 and 3:23–38 (the annunciations, the birth of John, the nativity, the shepherds, the circumcision and presentation, Simeon and Anna).
The Johannine prologue (the Logos hymn) — John 1:1–18. A theological hymn on the pre-incarnate Word, not narrated ministry; the canon's Johannine material begins at John 1:19 (the Baptist's testimony).
The single pre-ministry pericope retained is the boy Jesus in the temple (Luke 2:41–52, Prologue), kept as "unofficial ministry."
2. Post-ascension material
Excluded as falling beyond the terminus (the ascension: Mark 16:19; Luke 24:50–53; Acts 1:9–11).
The Acts of the Apostles, except the ministry summaries. The narrative of the early church (effectively Acts 2–28) reports the activity of the apostles, not the earthly ministry of Jesus. Retained from Acts: 1:1–11 (the forty days of teaching and the ascension boundary); the kerygmatic summaries of the ministry at 2:22–24, 10:36–43, and 13:23–31 (Appendix A); and the agraphon at 20:35 ("more blessed to give than to receive," Appendix B).
Revelation. An apocalyptic vision of the exalted Christ and the end. It is post-ascension and visionary rather than a report of the earthly Jesus; the words of the risen Christ to the seven churches (Rev 2–3) are given in vision after the ascension and so fall outside the window.
3. Interpretation and theology rather than reporting (the Epistles)
The letters expound the significance of Jesus; they do not narrate his deeds or teaching. They are excluded as a class, with the standing exception that any explicit dominical command or early reporting-formula they preserve is admitted at its place in the harmony.
Excluded in full: Romans; 2 Corinthians; Galatians; Ephesians; Philippians; Colossians; 2 Thessalonians; 1 and 2 Timothy; Titus; Philemon; Hebrews; James; 1 and 2 Peter; 1, 2, and 3 John; Jude.
Excluded except for the reporting fragments retained in the harmony:
1 Corinthians — retained: 7:10–11 (divorce, "not I, but the Lord"); 9:14 (the Lord's command that preachers be supported); 11:23–26 (the institution of the Supper); 15:3–7 (the resurrection-appearance formula).
1 Thessalonians — retained: 4:15–17 ("a word of the Lord" on the coming).
Two notes. The early confessional hymns — Philippians 2:6–11 and Colossians 1:15–20 — interpret Jesus rather than report him, and are not admitted even though they are pre-Pauline in origin. And James, which echoes Jesus's teaching more closely than any other letter, reports none of it as narrated dominical speech; it is excluded by the same rule as the rest.
4. Text-critical exclusions
Material absent from the working Greek text (Westcott-Hort 1881).
The Pericope Adulterae (the woman caught in adultery), John 7:53–8:11. Absent from the earliest and best manuscripts and relegated in Westcott-Hort; the harmony passes directly from John 7:52 to 8:12.
For completeness: the longer ending of Mark (16:9–20) and Luke 23:34a ("Father, forgive them") are likewise text-critically contested, but they are retained in the harmony with text-critical notes rather than omitted, and Luke 22:43–44 (the Gethsemane agony) falls within an included range. These are flagged, not left out.
On non-canonical material
This section concerns the New Testament. The early non-canonical texts that were considered and excluded — among them the resurrection-spectacle of Gospel of Peter §§34–49 (the giant figures and the talking cross), the Gospel of the Hebrews incarnation fragment, the catechetical and liturgical portions of the Didache (1:2–6; 8:2–3), and the seventeen excluded Gospel of Thomas logia — are documented at their points in the harmony (the ✕ directives) and, for Thomas, in the full triage at Appendix D.

The two appendices that follow (C, the placement table; and D, the Thomas triage) are editorial build apparatus, not part of the reader canon.

Appendix D — Gospel of Thomas Triage (all 114 logia)
One-time triage applying the project's two governing rules: (1) the whole-logion principle — if any clause is corroborated by the canonical/synoptic tradition, the entire logion enters as a unit, even where it carries an esoteric tail; (2) the similarity criterion — exclude material with little similarity to the Gospel Jesus or that is explicitly gnostic-cosmological (cosmogony, pre-existent images, ascent-passwords, the pleroma/"the All," make-male, flesh/spirit speculation).
Verdicts: A = anchored to a pericope (synoptic parallel); U = included but unanchored (coheres / reports a saying, no narrative home → Appendix B); X = excluded. "Firm" vs. "borderline" marks how secure the call is; borderline rows are the ones to review first. Gospel of Peter
Pilate and Herod
(1) But of the Jews no one washed the hands, neither Herod nor one of his judges, and when they didn't want to wash Pilate stood up. (2) And then Herod the king, commanding the Lord to be brought, said to them, "Do whatever I commanded you to do to him."
Joseph Requests Jesus' Body
(3) And Joseph stood there, the friend of Pilate and the Lord. And seeing that they were about to crucify him, he went to Pilate and asked for the body of the Lord for burial. (4) And Pilate, having sent to Herod, asked him for the body.
(5) And Herod said, "Brother Pilate, even if someone hadn't asked for him, we would've buried him, since also Sabbath is beginning, because it's written in the Law, 'The sun shouldn't set on one who's been killed.'"
The Lord is Tortured and Mocked
And he handed him over to the people before the first day of their festival of the Unleavened Bread. (6) And those having taken the Lord were running, pushing him, and saying, "Let's drag the Son of God, having authority over him!"
(7) And they were clothing him with purple, and sat him on the seat of judgment, saying, "Judge justly, king of Israel!" (8) And one of them brought a thorn crown and placed it on the Lord's head.
(9) And other bystanders were spitting in his face, and others slapped his cheeks. Others were piercing him with a reed, and some were scourging him, saying, "With this honor, let's honor the Son of God!"
The Lord is Crucified
(10) And they brought two wrongdoers and crucified the Lord in the middle of them, but he was silent as if having no pain. (11) And when they set up the cross, they wrote that "This is the king of Israel." (12) And having laid the clothes in front of him, they divided them and cast lots for them.
(13) But one of those wrongdoers rebuked them, saying, "We, because of the wrong that we did, are suffering this way, but this one, having become Savior of humanity, what wrong has he done to you?"
(14) And they were angry at him. They commanded that the legs not be broken, so that he might die tortured.
The Lord Dies
(15) And it was mid-day, and darkness held fast over all Judea. And they were troubled and distressed lest the sun set, since he was still living. It's written to them, "The sun shouldn't set on one who's been killed." (16) And one of them said, "Give him bile with sour wine to drink." And having mixed it, they gave it to him to drink. (17) And they fulfilled all things and accumulated the sins on their head. (18) And many were going around with lanterns, thinking it was night, and some fell down.
(19) And the Lord cried out, saying, "My Power, the Power, you've left me!" And when he said this, it was taken up. (20) And that very hour, the veil of the temple in Jerusalem was torn in two.
The Lord is Buried
(21) And then they drew the nails from the Lord's hands and placed him on the earth. And the whole earth was shaken, and great fear came.
(22) Then the sun shone and it was found to be the ninth hour. (23) And the Jews rejoiced, and gave Joseph his body to bury, since he had seen all the good he had done. (24) And having taken the Lord, he washed him and wrapped him in linen, and brought him into his own tomb, which was known as "Joseph's Garden."
People React

The Tomb is Secured
(28) And the scribes, the Pharisees, and the elders gathered together with one another, having heard that all the people were grumbling, beating their chests, and saying, "If at his death these greatest signs have happened, see how just he was!"

It is written in the Gospel of the Hebrews:
When Christ wished to come upon the earth to men, the good Father summoned a mighty power in heaven, which was called Michael, and entrusted Christ to the care thereof. And the power came into the world and it was called Mary, and Christ was in her womb seven months.(Cyril of Jerusalem, Discourse on Mary Theotokos 12a)
According to the Gospel written in the Hebrew speech, which the
Nazaraeans read, the whole fount of the Holy Spirit shall descend upon him. . . Further in the Gospel which we have just mentioned we find the following written:And it came to pass when the Lord was come up out of the water, the whole fount of the Holy Spirit descended upon him and rested on him and said to him: My son, in all the prophets was I waiting for thee that thou shouldest come and I might rest in thee. For thou art my rest; thou art my first-begotten Son that reignest for ever.(Jerome, Commentary on Isaiah 4 [on Isaiah 11:2])
And if any accept the Gospel of the Hebrews - here the Savior
says:Even so did my mother, the Holy Spirit, take me by one of my hairs and carry me away on to the great mountain Tabor.(Origen, Commentary on John 2.12.87 [on John 1:3])
As also it stands written in the Gospel of the Hebrews:
He that marvels shall reign, and he that has reigned shall rest.(Clement, Stromateis 2.9.45.5)To those words (from Plato, Timaeus 90) this is equivalent:He that seeks will not rest until he finds; and he that has found shall marvel; and he that has marvelled shall reign; and he that has reigned shall rest.(Ibid., 5.14.96.3)
As we have read in the Hebrew Gospel, the Lord says to his
disciples:And never be ye joyful, save when ye behold your brother with love.(Jerome, Commentary on Ephesians 3 [on Ephesians 5:4])
In the Gospel according to the Hebrews, which the Nazaraeans are
wont to read, there is counted among the most grievous offences:He that has grieved the spirit of his brother.(Jerome, Commentary on Ezekiel 6 [on Ezekiel 18:7])
The Gospel called according to the Hebrews which was recently
translated by me into Greek and Latin, which Origen frequently uses, records after the resurrection of the Savior:And when the Lord had given the linen cloth to the servant of the priest, he went to James and appeared to him. For James had sworn that he would not eat bread from that hour in which he had drunk the cup of the Lord until he should see him risen from among them that sleep. And shortly thereafter the Lord said: Bring a table and bread! And immediately it added: he took the bread, blessed it and brake it and gave it to James the Just and said to him: My brother, eat thy bread, for the Son of man is risen from among them that sleep.(Jerome, De viris inlustribus 2)
P.Oxy 840
Preface
In December 1905—the sixth season at Oxyrhynchus—Grenfell and Hunt registered the discovery of a very small parchment book measuring approximately 3.3 by 2.75 inches (8.5 x 7 cm). Such a relic was likely valued as much as an amulet as for its content, which was written in the tiniest of scripts. The contents, however, turned out to be quite interesting: parts of two episodes from a heretofore-unknown gospel, the identity of which remains a mystery to this day. The manuscript is housed in the Bodleian Library at Oxford. In the first episode, we hear Jesus warning the disciples against imitating certain “evildoers,” who are to be punished in this life and in the next. In the second episode, Jesus and his disciples wander into the Temple precinct and are confronted by a Pharisaic priest.
